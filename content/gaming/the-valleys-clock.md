The cemetery at the foot of the castle road is the most quietly significant feature of the village of Barovia. By day it is unremarkable. At midnight the procession of one hundred spirits rises from its graves, walks the road to the castle, climbs the chapel staircase to the high tower, and throws itself down the shaft toward the crypts. The module describes them as "the spirits of previous adventurers who died trying to destroy Strahd," repeating the attempt nightly and failing nightly. The procession is not atmosphere. It is a register.

This article reads the procession as a ledger, considers what its count lets us infer about the rate at which expeditions have arrived in the valley, and traces the consequences of that rate through the village's demographic and behavioural condition. The Census of the Damned established what is in the valley now. The economic study established how the valley sustains itself as a closed system. The present article is the temporal axis: how the valley arrived at its present state across the span of Strahd's reign, and what mechanism the procession lets us infer has been running to produce it.

## The procession as ledger

I6 supplies the figures the calculation needs. The recommended party size is six to eight player characters at the fifth to seventh levels of experience. The procession contains one hundred spirits, identified by the module as previous adventurers killed in the attempt on Strahd. The Optional Ending dates the cycle: Tatyana and Sergei are reunited "after 500 years," which fixes the duration of Strahd's existence as the valley's master at half a millennium.

Taking the cycle's full length and dividing by a generational interval of twenty-five years gives twenty turns of the cycle. Taking four hundred years instead gives sixteen. The procession's hundred spirits, divided across that span, yield roughly five spirits per generation of cycle. Each expedition averages six to eight adventurers, so the average generation leaves something like a party's majority behind in the cemetery and a minority alive somewhere in the valley. The procession is not the count of failed expeditions. It is the count of deaths sustained by them, with the survivors absorbed elsewhere.

This is the article's first inferential step and the rest follows from it. The procession records heroic deaths, specifically those who died attempting to destroy Strahd. It does not record adventurers killed on the road by wolves before reaching the castle. It does not record those who died in ambush at Tser Pool or on the bridges. It does not record the survivors of failed expeditions who could not leave the valley because the fog had closed behind them. Each of these categories sits outside the procession's accounting and must be reconstructed from elsewhere in the valley's evidence.

## The survivor pathway

The fog is the mechanism that can convert failed expeditions into permanent valley population. The module is explicit. The fog at the borders chokes anyone attempting to leave who has not been given Strahd's potion, and the potion is held by Madam Eva and is unobtainable. Anyone who walks into the valley walks in once. The fog does not distinguish between adventurers, merchants, refugees, or mercenaries, nor between the heroic and the broken. A survivor of a failed expedition who has lost his companions, exhausted his supplies, and watched his cleric's holy symbol prove insufficient is in exactly the same position as any other person who has crossed the border. He cannot leave.

He therefore stays. He has the option of dying on his own terms, which some take. He has the option of joining the village's population, which the article assumes some take, because the alternatives in the valley are limited. He cannot become a Rhenari because the Rhenari are a closed kinship group. He cannot serve in the castle because the castle's living population is Strahd's selected dependents. The village is the residual category. He arrives at one of the houses, finds it occupied or not, takes shelter, and over time becomes a Barovian by every functional measure except origin.

Over twenty generations of cycle, this absorption rate produces a small but steady infusion of outsider lineages into the village. If the party-size assumption holds, each expedition contributes between one and three survivors on average. Across the cycle's full span, the cumulative arrival is perhaps thirty to fifty individuals across half a millennium. That is small in absolute terms, and small enough that it does not fundamentally alter the village's character. It is also large enough to be visible in the surname field, which is the section of this argument the Bildrath case study addresses below.

## The population trajectory

The Census established the village's current living population at approximately two hundred, derived from the building count and the occupancy probabilities the module supplies. Working back from that figure to the cycle's beginning requires an estimate of the starting population, and I6 does not state one. The estimate must be reconstructed from the visible decay.

The module describes a village in which most of the shops are permanently closed, the unoccupied stores have been looted and ruined, claw marks cover most of the walls, and the houses are 60% occupied with one man and a 30% chance of his wife and a 10% chance of children. The architecture indicates a community that once supported considerably more commerce and population than it currently sustains. The Burgomaster's mansion was built when there was something to administer. The church has a bell tower built when the bell was rung for a community large enough to need calling. The mercantile occupies a building seventy feet long by forty feet wide, sized for a customer base that has long since stopped existing. None of this can be made into a precise figure. A village whose current condition is two hundred and whose architectural footprint suggests several times that, at the high end of plausible reconstruction, indicates a starting population in the high hundreds. Eight hundred is a reasonable midpoint estimate. Six hundred and a thousand both fit the visible evidence. The article will use the eight hundred figure as a working number and acknowledge that the reconstruction supports a range.

The decline from approximately eight hundred to approximately two hundred across five centuries is a net loss of six hundred people. Across twenty generations of cycle, that is an average net loss of thirty per generation. The cycle's expeditions contribute a small positive offset of one to three survivors per generation, so the gross loss from native lineages is closer to thirty-three per generation. Half of those, roughly, would be expected from natural mortality at pre-modern rates in a community that is reproducing at replacement. The other half is the cycle's specific contribution: deaths from Strahd's harassment, deaths from wolves, deaths from the village's quietly compounding inability to sustain itself.

The mechanism that converts this into a self-reinforcing decline is the article's interpretive crux, and the next section addresses it as inference rather than as enumeration.

## The sex-skew compound

The mechanism that converts gradual decline into self-reinforcing collapse is, on the article's reading, sex-selective predation. This is the most inferentially loaded section of the argument and should be marked as such. The module does not state that Strahd preferentially targets women across the village population, and the conclusion is not one the text forces. It is, however, consistent with the evidence the text supplies, and the article advances it as an interpretation that fits rather than as a finding the module encodes.

The evidence is as follows. Strahd's goals, as elaborated in the Fortunes of Ravenloft, repeatedly centre on a female target. The bride goal is the most explicit. Tatyana's incarnations are female and the cycle of pursuit across centuries has been organised around her. Ireena is the current incarnation. Gertruda is in the bedchamber at K42, taken from her mother and held against later use. The named female victims and quasi-victims in the village's present condition are consistent with a pattern in which Strahd's specific predations target women. The article's inference is that this pattern, generalised across five centuries of harassment, would have removed women from the village's reproductive base at a faster rate than men.

The 60/30/10 occupancy pattern is the residue this inference predicts. Sixty percent of houses occupied. Of those, each contains one man. Only thirty percent of those men have their wives still present. Only ten percent of those households have children. The pattern is consistent with what a population skewed toward men by sustained selective pressure on women would look like. It is also consistent with other readings: a community in late-stage household fragmentation from any cause, a population that has lost women to ordinary mortality at higher rates than men because of childbirth complications across centuries, or a village whose women have been the more frequent emigrants in the period before the fog closed. The article's preferred reading is the sex-skewed predation reading because it integrates the household pattern with Strahd's own targeting behaviour, but the alternative readings are not foreclosed by the text.

If the inference holds, it compounds across time in a way uniform predation does not. A balanced population can absorb a generation of losses and recover from the surviving young, because the surviving young include both sexes in roughly equal proportions. A sex-skewed population cannot, because the surviving young are themselves skewed. The next generation's reproductive pairs are limited not by the total survivors but by the number of women among them. Even a generation of complete cessation of Strahd's predation would not correct the ratio under this reading, because the missing women would not return and the parental pool that would have produced the next generation's daughters would have already been depleted.

On this reading the valley declines on a ratchet. Each turn of the cycle removes more women than men, the next generation has fewer mothers than the previous, the generation after that has fewer still, and the rate of decline accelerates rather than stabilising. The eight-hundred-to-two-hundred trajectory across five centuries is what this ratchet would produce if it held. The cycle would, on this reading, have run long enough to reduce the village to the threshold below which sustained reproduction is doubtful.

The article emphasises that this section is interpretation rather than enumeration. The procession count and the 60/30/10 pattern are textual. The connection between them through sex-selective predation is the article's reading.

## The behavioural lock-in

The village's response to this is not despair, though it resembles despair from outside. It is the rational extrapolation from a long run of identical prior cases.

The Barovians have watched expeditions arrive across many generations. Madam Eva, whom I6 describes as having "seen a good many" adventurers in her time, is the Rhenari record of the same pattern from a different vantage. The villagers have observed the expeditions, observed their failures, observed their compositions and outcomes, and learned what those outcomes are. The empirical record is unanimous. Expeditions fail. Helpers of expeditions fail with them. The village does not benefit from the expeditions' attempts because the expeditions do not succeed. Helping the expedition is therefore not a strategy with positive expected value for the village. It is a strategy with consistently negative expected value, learned over twenty generations.

A few villagers have attempted to act despite this. Some men, reaching the point where the conditions of the valley become unbearable, have presumably left their houses with the intention of doing something. A small number of those would have been killed by Strahd's nightly attentions before they accomplished anything. A small number would have attached themselves to passing expeditions as guides, porters, or fighters, contributing to the procession's count alongside the outsiders if the expedition was destroyed. A small number would have survived such attempts and returned to their houses, having seen what the expedition saw and learned what the expedition learned. A villager who has watched an expedition die in the castle does not advertise the experience, does not lead a second expedition, does not attempt to rouse the village. He confirms the village's accumulated lesson and goes back to his door.

The men in the houses who refuse to engage with the party are therefore not failing to recognise an opportunity. They are correctly recognising a pattern. The pattern's content is that this expedition will fail like the previous expeditions, that helping the expedition will accelerate the helper's removal from the village, and that the only sustainable response to a recurring fatal event is to minimise exposure to it. The villagers cower because cowering is the survival strategy with the longest empirical track record in the valley.

Donavich is the polar case. He has chosen the resistance strategy that maximises personal cost for marginal communal benefit. His prayers maintain the church's protection nightly, at the cost of his own condition: hoarse, weakened, exhausted, with ten hit points against an adversary who has been wearing him down for as long as he has been the priest. The contrast with the village's quietism is sharp, and the sharpness is the point. Both responses are coherent. The village's response minimises further loss. Donavich's response maximises personal sacrifice for communal good. The village has, by majority, chosen the strategy with the better track record. Donavich has, alone, chosen the strategy that requires more of him than any individual can sustain indefinitely. His exhaustion is the visible cost of choosing the rarer path.

Bildrath is the village's quietism in a particular professional form. A merchant who served only Barovians would have starved generations ago, because Barovians have nothing to trade and no money to pay. A merchant who serves the Rhenari and the periodic adventuring party is one who has found the only sustainable commercial niche the valley contains. He is not callous. The valley's crisis is not solvable by anything Bildrath sells, and engaging with it would only get him taken too. His ten-times-book pricing is the trade-route premium for serving a population whose other source of supply is the procession's stock returning down the road via the Rhenari. The Census's economic chapter took this further. The article's interest in Bildrath is narrower: he is the village's quietism doing what it does in commercial form, and he is the case study for the surname argument that follows.

## The surname evidence as case study

The Barovian naming field is consistent. Kolyan, Indirovich, Ireena, Ismark, Kolyana, Donavich, and the Christian name Mary all sit within recognisable Slavic or Eastern European phonology. Gertruda is Germanic but within the field of names that Slavic populations adopted. Arik is short and unmarked enough to fit anywhere. The names belong to the field a Carpathian-coded valley would produce.

Bildrath does not. The name reads more Germanic-fantasy than Slavic-folk, and it sits awkwardly in the village's onomastic context. Parriwimple, Bildrath's stock-boy, is also off-pattern, but in a different direction; the name is clearly a humour-name with no pretence of fitting any naming field. Bildrath sits in a third category. It is foreign-sounding without being a joke, and its foreignness is consistent with the phonology of a name that entered the valley from elsewhere.

The article advances this as a case study illustrating the survivor pathway rather than as evidence for it. The claim is that *if* the survivor pathway operates as the article has described, *then* Bildrath is the kind of name the pathway would produce, and his commercial profile is the kind of profile the pathway would select for. The case study is illustrative texture, not proof.

The illustrative reading runs as follows. The current Bildrath is a Barovian merchant, alive in the present generation, with all the local knowledge and local skills the role requires. He is not centuries old. The name, however, is not native to the valley. The most economical explanation, given the survivor pathway's existence as a mechanism, is that the name entered the village some generations ago through an outsider who arrived with an expedition, survived, and remained. Across the intervening generations the family would have assimilated linguistically and culturally while retaining the surname as a vestige of the founding event. This is how surnames work in any settled population. The phonology of a name freezes at the moment a family establishes itself in a community and drifts only slowly thereafter, even as the family becomes indistinguishable from its neighbours by every other measure.

A founding ancestor of the type the survivor pathway would deposit is more likely to have been a non-combatant attached to an expedition than a frontline fighter. A quartermaster, a hireling, a merchant attached to the expedition for logistical support, or a non-combatant of any kind is more likely to have survived an expedition's destruction than a fighter or a cleric. The current Bildrath's commercial instinct and his lack of any visible adventurer skills are consistent with descent from such a profile, and the mercantile's founding stock would, on this reading, have included the recovered gear of his ancestor's dead companions, which is the same source of supply the current Bildrath continues to operate from via the Rhenari.

The reading does not foreclose alternatives. The Hickmans likely chose the name as a fantasy-shopkeeper sound, possibly with a faint pun on the Germanic *Bild* and *Rat* elements, and that authorial intent is the simplest explanation for the name's foreignness. The article's claim is narrower than that the descent reading is correct: the claim is that the descent reading is consistent with the survivor pathway and illustrates how the pathway would manifest if it operates. Stronger claims would require evidence the module does not provide.

The procession is the cycle's standing record. It walks the same route every night because the cycle repeats the same pattern every time. Each notional cohort of spirits represents a turn of the cycle in which Tatyana returned, an expedition arrived, the expedition failed, the dead joined the procession, and the survivors were absorbed into the village's slow demographic decline. The pattern has run for five centuries and has produced exactly the valley I6 describes: a shrinking population, a fragmented household structure, a quietism learned across twenty generations of failed action, a closed economy rotating expedition gear back through the mercantile, and a cemetery whose nightly traffic is the visible record of every previous attempt at the destruction the current party is about to attempt.

The party walking into the village is the next entry in this ledger. The Fortunes reading is Madam Eva telling them where they fit in the pattern. The cemetery, on the night they watch it, is showing them what their own procession will look like if they fail. The villagers' refusal to engage is not abandonment of the party. It is the villagers protecting themselves from a recurrence whose outcome they have observed many times.

The cycle has only two terminations. The party either succeeds, in which case Strahd is destroyed, the fog dissolves, the valley reopens, and the cycle ends; or the party fails, in which case the procession gains its next cohort, the village absorbs whatever survivors there are, and the valley waits for the next incarnation. There is no third option. The cycle does not stabilise, the valley does not recover gradually, and the village's decline does not reverse without an external break in the pattern. The party's arrival is the only mechanism by which the cycle can be broken, and this is true of every previous expedition that has walked the same road into the village.

The valley has been waiting. The procession has been counting. The village has been declining. The current expedition belongs to roughly the twentieth turn of the cycle, and the only one that matters is the one that succeeds, because the pattern terminates with that success and not with any of the failures that have preceded it.

That is the valley's clock. It runs on the Tatyana cycle, it deposits its generations into the cemetery, and it has been ticking for five centuries with the same outcome each time. Whether it ticks once more without changing, or whether the present cohort of adventurers is the cohort that breaks it, is the question I6 leaves to the table.
